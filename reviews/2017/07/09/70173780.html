A good little camera, but with issues

  • ProfHankD
  • 2017/07/09

I'll start by admitting that I'm not a fan of MFT. I actually like a lot of small-sensor fixed-lens compacts, but for interchangeable lenses, I like to shoot with manual-focus lenses that were mostly designed for 135-film SLRs, and MFT makes none of them wide angle and challenges the resolution a little too much. However, despite the ironic aspect-ratio mismatch of a 4:3 sensor for 16:9 capture, MFT cameras seem to be pretty well tuned for video... and the DC-GX850 not only shoots 4K, but also has a mode shooting 8MP stills at 30FPS -- and that's why I bought this.
Ergonomically, the camera is a very mixed bag. The menu system is very straightforward and provides intuitive access to a wide range of features, such as fairly flexible raw development. The touch screen is used fairly well too. On the other hand, you need the touch screen and diving into menus more on this camera than on most because the buttons can't directly do much of what you need. The same goes for the body design; it's small and solid with a selfie-capable flip-up LCD, but it's actually pretty awkward to hold even with the tiny collapsible kit zoom lens, let alone with my bigger SLR lenses. You can get a somewhat better grip by flipping the LCD up a bit and holding under it, but it can be very difficult to see the LCD in daylight that way.
The kit zoom covers a good focal range and seems optically sound. Here's a sample shot (through a slightly rained-on window):

That's fairly pleasing, despite being shot at a challenging-for-MFT 1250 ISO. However, that's not what the scene looked like -- it wasn't so green (and there's a lot of smeary noise reduction too). I shot that RAW+JPEG, so let's also see what the raw looks like with pretty much default RawTherapy processing:

Wow! That might be the biggest improvement from using raw that I've seen with any camera made in the past decade. DPReview said raw really helps with this camera and I absolutely agree; the JPEGs actually don't compete all that well even against my $130 Canon sx530hs superzoom, but the raws blow it away.
Can it make good images with a legacy lens? Yeah, if the lens is good enough in the MFT crop area. Focus peaking is a little too subtle, but it's precise. Here's a shot (from raw) taken with my Canon FL 55mm f/1.2 wide open:

This lens actually does better wide open on APS-C than on FF, where corners get dark. It resolves well enough in the center for MFT even wide open, but the SA (halo around the front flower) and axial CA (green fringe on the rear flower) are more obvious than on 24MP APS-C -- as one would expect due to the slightly higher 16MP MFT pixel density.
Now it's time to get to the really bad issues.... I expected to use this body mostly with manual lenses, but it will not allow use of the mechanical shutter with them! Why not? I don't know; it makes no sense and this fact isn't mentioned in any of the documentation for the camera. You still get the 60s to 1/16000s full shutter speed range, but all with (silent, unless you let it play one of the shutter sound recordings, which it does by default) fully electronic shutter. Rolling electronic shutter.
It has half of a mechanical focal plane shutter -- the second curtain, so it only could do electronic first curtain (EFC). The mechanical shutter is also an unusual stepper-driven design (about 1/100s traversal) and for whatever reason the camera only allows it to be used for between 1/60s and 1/500s. However, the camera doesn't let you use the mechanical shutter at all with manual lenses! Instead, you get a rolling electronic shutter with a traversal time around 1/25s.
Ok, so we'd expect a little jello in videos; so what? Well, what you get in stills is spatial distortion of anything that 1/25s wouldn't freeze -- no matter how high you set the shutter speed. So what? Well, with manual lenses suffering a 2X (actually more than 2X if you don't want 4:3 aspect) crop, a lousy grip that enhances shake, and no IBIS to steady shake this causes real problems. In other words, unless you're adapting a lens with a focal length less than about 25mm/2 = 12mm or have amazingly steady hands, expect spatial distortion. That said, you didn't see it in the 1/1000s flower shot above, did you? That's because the flowers don't have long straight lines, etc., that would make the distortion more obvious... and I have steady hands, gripped the camera with the LCD flipped up (enough to get my hand behind it), and the flowers were not moving. It also isn't a huge problem with native lenses that have anti-shake or are used with EFC.
Now to the 4K photo mode. Really nicely implemented from a user's point of view -- including a circular buffer mode that continuously shoots and then saves images from before and after you pressed the shutter button. However, it doesn't actually shoot stills in 4K photo modes. What it really does is capture video and let you extract frames, although it hides that fact pretty well. This means electronic shutter only, no raws, etc. Still a really great feature, but not quite as great as it at first sounds.
In summary, this is a very nice little camera if you don't mind lack of an EVF and will be using native lenses with anti-shake built-into them. JPEGs are ok and colorful, but you can do much better with raws. The small sensor actually does fine in image quality -- it's the wacky shutter restrictions (the irony of 1/16000s pixel samples taken over 1/25s), crop factor, poor grip, and lack of IBIS that really limit what you can do with this camera. If you're looking to use MTF with manual lenses, better to get a body that will let you use EFC and ideally also has IBIS... or get a Sony APS-C body (the vastly superior A6000 body is about the same cost as this with kit zoom).
